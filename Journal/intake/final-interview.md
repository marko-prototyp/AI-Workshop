# Final interview — for the participant case study

> Use this in week 10 or 11 with each participant individually. 30–45 minutes, recorded if possible. Their answers become the spine of the long-form case study they take home as their "certificate."
>
> The case study is in their voice — first-person — so capture how they actually talk, not how a press release would.

## 1. Pitch your project in one sentence — the way you'd say it to a designer at a conference.

> Don't hedge. Don't add "kind of" or "I'm trying to". One clean sentence.



## 2. Why this project, for you specifically?

> Not the brief — the personal reason. What pissed you off, what you wanted to use, what you wanted to prove. Be honest.



## 3. What's the most important design decision you made?

> Could be a constraint you set, a thing you chose not to build, a reference you picked, an interaction you fought for. The one decision that shaped everything else.



## 4. What surprised you about working with Claude?

> Specific moment. Could be good or bad. Pretend you're telling a friend — not writing a LinkedIn post.



## 5. What did you fight against?

> The friction. The thing that kept going wrong. The week you almost gave up. Real designers respect this section more than the wins.



## 6. What changed about how you work, day to day?

> Not "AI is now part of my workflow" — actual specifics. What you do differently on a Tuesday because of these 12 weeks.



## 7. What does this project do that nothing else does?

> The point of view. The thing it argues. If it doesn't argue anything, that's a problem — let's name it now and decide what to do about it.



## 8. What's still wrong with it?

> Real designers list this in their case studies. It builds trust. Three things, minimum.



## 9. What would you build next, if you had another 12 weeks?

> A direction, not a feature list. Where the work wants to go.



## 10. The screenshot that best represents the work.

> Just one. We'll use it as the lead image.



## 11. One sentence on how it felt.

> The whole thing. Not how it went — how it felt.
