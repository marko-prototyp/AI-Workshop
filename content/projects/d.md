---
letter: "D"
name: "A playful microsite"
desc: "A concept-driven one-page site where the writing or idea does as much work as the visual design."
bestFor: "creative range"
pickIf: |
  you want creative range over polish. A manifesto, a "things I learned in 10 years" essay, a generative piece tied to your work — something where the writing or concept is doing as much work as the visual design.
goesWrong: |
  The concept is too small. "A site about my favorite fonts" isn't a concept; it's a list. The concept needs to have a take. "A site arguing that designers should stop using Inter" is a concept. Strong concepts have a thesis, even if the thesis is playful.
goodLooks: |
  You leave the site quoting it. The visual design and the writing are doing the same job — they argue the same point. There's a structural surprise somewhere: the navigation does something unexpected, the scroll behavior is part of the joke, the type changes mid-essay to make a point. It feels like a piece of writing you'd send to a friend, not a design exercise.
trap: |
  Microsites are where design references can hurt you most. The web is full of similar-looking "playful" sites. Pull from print: zines, indie magazines, gallery catalogs, manifestos, posters. Pull from books: typography in pre-digital paperbacks. The freshest microsites look least like other microsites.
---
WORKING TITLE
Try writing it as a sentence, not a noun phrase. "Stop using Inter"
beats "Inter." Titles with a verb tend to have stronger concepts.

THE CONCEPT IN ONE SENTENCE
"It's a [format] arguing that [thesis]." If there's no thesis,
keep going.
Examples:
- "It's a manifesto arguing that designers should stop pretending
  AI doesn't exist."
- "It's a generative piece that visualizes every font I've used
  this year, weighted by how often."
- "It's an essay disguised as a horoscope, where each sign is a
  type of designer."

WHO IS THIS FOR
Yourself counts. So does "other designers who feel the same way."
Be honest — it'll show in the writing voice.

THE STRUCTURE
What are the sections, in order? Even rough. A microsite without
a structure becomes one long scroll of unrelated thoughts. Five
to nine sections is a good range.

THE STRUCTURAL SURPRISE
What's the unexpected interactive or visual move? Microsites need
one thing the reader didn't see coming. The type that changes
mid-essay. The nav that's also the table of contents. The scroll
that goes sideways. Pick one — only one.

THE VOICE
A paragraph in the actual voice of the site, written now. If you
can't write it now, the voice isn't decided. Examples of voice
adjectives: dry, earnest, sardonic, breathless, academic-
pretending-not-to-be-academic, deliberately overconfident.

THREE REFERENCES, NONE OF THEM TECH
Magazines, books, posters, zines, museum catalogues, indie press.
Name them.

WHAT IT'S NOT
- It's NOT a portfolio.
- It's NOT a tutorial or how-to.
- It's NOT trying to be useful. It's trying to be read.
