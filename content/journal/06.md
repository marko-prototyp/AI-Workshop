Three designers wrote the most complete briefs of the program and handed them to Claude Design. One comp held. Two came back as stock.

The session had a fork. Comp the screen in Figma and hand Claude the screenshot, or describe it straight to Claude Design. Nobody picked Figma, and the brief held better in Claude Design than the same prompt does in an ordinary chat. Each person did two things first: fold every planning doc into one design system, and turn their anti-stock rules into a reusable skill. Then they ran a hardened brief for one screen. That brief was meant to be the payoff. For two of three, it wasn't.

## Wattlog, the brief the tool wouldn't hold.

Ivo folded five planning docs and Session 05's decisions into one design system: about 580 lines covering art direction, banned moves, the token layer, content libraries, and a redraw recipe for each of the ten screens. He scoped the skill to Wattlog only and hardened the Today-screen brief into one paste-ready block, naming the three places accent could appear and the six banned moves most likely to break there. He also locked the form factor: narrow web, about 680px, the decision left open since the wireframes.

Then the brief went into Claude Design and the comp didn't hold. Ivo got an acceptable result only after building the full design system inside Claude Design and applying it whole. The better version came from a different route, an outside material-design skill, which produced the cleanest comp anyone made that day. His rule for a brief like this: *"leave as little unspecified space as possible."* He's dropping Claude Design as the comp tool, since it wouldn't hold the brief. Whether a custom design system earns its keep is still open.

## ClassArcade, the comp that lost its character.

Paula made her skill, reworked the visual direction, and built a first version of the main view, "Today's roster." In chat she flipped the leaderboard podium to 1·2·3 order and resolved a contradiction in the brief: the point number is always the largest, heaviest thing on a row, and the name stays the most expressive. She folded the project, visual, and Week-4 briefs into one design system and flagged eight underspecified items, type scale, spacing, flash colors, button color, for sign-off instead of letting the model guess.

The comp was the disappointment. The dashboard lost the character the brief had given it, and Claude kept repeating the same move after she asked it not to. Getting that character back is what sent us looking for a better way to work, and we found one after the session.

## Naučimo Hrvatski, the one that held.

Marin's comp held the brief. He folded four planning docs into one design system, locked the type scale to "Balanced," and reconciled a conflict between two briefs over the glass recipe. His skill held strictly to pasted constraints with no invented defaults. The first comp of the Scene screen, "Sarma kod bake," came back the way he asked: Marin as a text pill, chips as warm tiles with no green or red, a warm toggle instead of a blue one, and a gold-bordered Nastavi rather than a filled Submit.

He also made the session's biggest structural calls, most of them clearing last session's gap list. The app drops the separate AI tutor and is written in first person, as the real Marin showing Kenzie his photos. Typing is gone, replaced by dragging the right word chip into a blank, because typing Croatian felt like homework to a beginner. A wrong chip now shows what that word means before she retries, so a rejection becomes a lesson instead of a gate. The finale is dragging full sentences into order, a real boss level, and the vocab shelf is reachable mid-chapter, with the ability to flip back to earlier photos. His line: *"I'm satisfied with how Claude was steering me."* Next he'll organize the content and let Cowork pull it from his Apple Notes.

The briefs were better than they'd ever been, and the tool still flattened two of three. The one that held was the project with the most specific visual language, which restates the visual-direction finding: vague leaves the model room to average, and Marin's brief left it none.

The fix turned up after the session, and it wasn't a longer brief. It was a skill. We took a Material Design skill template, fed it image references for the look we wanted, and had Claude rewrite it into a ClassArcade skill. The skill carries the whole direction: Fraunces serif against Space Grotesk, one acid-green accent over black-and-white, a hairline roster table instead of a stack of cards, monochrome initials instead of emoji. With it, Paula rebuilt the main view and a player profile in near-perfect sync, and it's now the source of truth for how ClassArcade looks. That's the move the program keeps arriving at: hand the model the constraints up front instead of describing them. It probably changes how the build phase runs. Ivan wasn't here again.
