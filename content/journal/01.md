Three designers showed up. Each came in with a project that already had a real user — in two cases, the designer themselves.

## The participants

**Ivo Anić — Personal Cycling & Training Dashboard.** A web tool that ingests CSV exports from Garmin, power meter, and HR monitor and turns them into power zones, training load, ride history, and trends. Engineering mindset applied to athletic data. Dieter Rams as a stance, not a style.

**Ivan Komlenić — Mockup Generator.** Upload a graphic, dial in a feeling across six pre-engineered dimensions, get a realistic mockup. Four clicks, one generation, no text fields. The interface does the prompt engineering the user shouldn't have to.

**Paula — Classroom Behavior Tracker.** A mobile-first tool one specific teacher will open during real classes to log behavior across focus, participation, and disruption. Weekly AI summaries arrive in week 10. The interaction design has to work first.

## The pattern

All three arrived with the same instinct — *build for a real problem, not a portfolio brief.* No concepts. Each has a named success condition involving real use by a real person.

There was also shared scope discipline. Each participant independently named what they were *not* building and why — live API integration, custom model training, complex backend. That's unusual for week one. Usually the constraint conversation happens in week three when ambition collides with the time budget. This group did it themselves.

The aesthetics are different — Ivo's data-dense utilitarianism, Ivan's deliberately obvious UI, Paula's playful mobile tool. The premise underneath is the same: **specificity over decoration.** Tools with a point of view.

## Risks worth naming early

Ivo's "data-dense" direction needs an early visual test. Dense and readable is a hard target.

Ivan deferred the "bad generation" fallback. That's reasonable — but it'll quietly become the project in weeks five through seven, when the interface has to do the work the user isn't.

Paula's week-10 AI summary layer is the easiest thing to over-promise. "Surfaces patterns" is a high claim. We watch the framing.
