Three designers showed up. Each came in with a project that already had a real user. In two cases, that user was themselves.

## The three projects.

**Ivo: Personal Cycling & Training Dashboard.** A web tool that ingests CSV exports from Garmin, power meter, and HR monitor and turns them into power zones, training load, ride history, and trends. Engineering mindset applied to athletic data. Dieter Rams as a stance, not a style.

**Ivan: Mockup Generator.** Upload a graphic, dial in a feeling across six pre-engineered dimensions, get a realistic mockup. Four clicks, one generation, no text fields. The interface does the prompt engineering the user shouldn't have to.

**Paula: Classroom Behavior Tracker.** A mobile-first tool one specific teacher will open during real classes to log behavior across focus, participation, and disruption. AI summaries arrive in session 10. The interaction design has to work first.

## What they had in common.

All three arrived with the same instinct: *build for a real problem, not a portfolio brief.* No concepts. Each one already knew what success looked like and who it was for.

Each person independently named what they were *not* building and why. No live API integration, no custom model training, no complex backend. That conversation usually happens in session three when ambition hits the time budget. This group did it in session one.

The aesthetics are different. Ivo's going data-dense, Ivan is deliberately obvious, Paula's building something playful and mobile. The premise underneath is the same: **specificity over decoration.** Tools with a point of view.

## Risks we named early.

Ivo's "data-dense" direction needs an early visual test. Dense and readable is a hard target.

Ivan deferred the "bad generation" fallback. That's reasonable. It'll quietly become the project in sessions five through seven, when the interface has to do the work the user isn't.

Paula's session-10 AI summary layer is the easiest thing to over-promise. "Surfaces patterns" is a high claim. We watch the framing.

## There was a fourth project running in parallel.

While the three set their briefs, the facilitator was running a meta-project. This website, this journal, the workshop structure itself. The same session-01 lesson applied. First-draft copy was 100% AI-generated and the designer in the room could tell within seconds. By v3 it started to not suck.

The bigger decision this week was structural. Ivan's project needed real planning, so he went to talk to Seba, our dev who already shipped Photato (an AI project of his own). From here on, agency experts join the workshop when a project needs them. More devs along the way. A tester too.
