Three designers showed up. A fourth ran the handbook solo at his own desk and asked to join after. Each came in with a project that already had a real user. Two of them, the designer themselves. Two of them, someone they love.

## The four projects.

**Ivo: Personal Cycling & Training Dashboard.** A web tool that ingests CSV exports from Garmin, power meter, and HR monitor and turns them into power zones, training load, ride history, and trends. Engineering mindset applied to athletic data. Dieter Rams as a stance, not a style.

**Ivan: Mockup Generator.** Upload a graphic, dial in a feeling across six pre-engineered dimensions, get a realistic mockup. Four clicks, one generation, no text fields. The interface does the prompt engineering the user shouldn't have to.

**Paula: Classroom Behavior Tracker.** A mobile-first tool one specific teacher will open during real classes to log behavior across focus, participation, and disruption. AI summaries arrive in session 10. The interaction design has to work first.

**Marin: Naučimo Hrvatski.** A language-learning app for one specific person. His girlfriend, in Philadelphia, learning Croatian for her eventual move to Osijek. Five scenarios mirror the actual FaceTime calls they have. An AI plays a Croatian boyfriend character, replies in Croatian with English in brackets, corrects gently. Not Duolingo. Built around one real relationship.

## What they had in common.

All four arrived with the same instinct: *build for a real problem, not a portfolio brief.* No concepts. Each one already knew what success looked like and who it was for.

Three of them independently named what they were *not* building, and why. No live API integration, no custom model training, no complex backend. That conversation usually happens in session three when ambition hits the time budget. They did it in session one. Marin ran the session solo so he didn't have the group conversation, which means the scope-naming pass is the first thing we do with him before session 02.

The aesthetics are different. Ivo's going data-dense, Ivan is deliberately obvious, Paula's building something playful and mobile. Marin's visual direction is still forming. The premise underneath is the same: **specificity over decoration.** Tools with a point of view.

## Risks we named early.

Ivo's "data-dense" direction needs an early visual test. Dense and readable is a hard target.

Ivan deferred the "bad generation" fallback. That's reasonable. It'll quietly become the project in sessions five through seven, when the interface has to do the work the user isn't.

Paula's session-10 AI summary layer is the easiest thing to over-promise. "Surfaces patterns" is a high claim. We watch the framing.

Marin's "better than Duolingo" framing is the easiest claim to over-promise too. The honest version is narrower and stronger. Duolingo doesn't have Croatian. *Naučimo Hrvatski* is built around one real relationship. That's the project. We sharpen the framing before session 03.

## The facilitator's project, running in parallel.

While the participants set their briefs, the facilitator was running a meta-project. This website. The build followed the same steps from the workshop's own curriculum, and produced a first-draft version of the site you're reading. The designers caught the AI-generated copy within seconds, so rewriting it became the priority. Only by v3 and v4 did it stop feeling salesy and sloppy.

Ivan's project needed real planning too, so we encouraged him to talk to Seba, our dev who shipped Photato. Photato runs on an image-generation API. Seba walked Ivan through how it's wired, and Ivan came back with a sharper sense of the technical surface. From here on, we'll invite agency experts to join the workshop when a project needs them.
