By the end of it, every project had a brief and a visual direction they could defend. None of the three designers were fully satisfied with what they'd made. That gap between "brief" and "proud of it" was the session's real subject.

The iteration pattern was the same across all three projects. Claude produced three initial directions. The designers rejected what felt like defaults, went to Pinterest, found things they actually liked, and brought those references back in. The second and third passes were better. None of them quite escaped Claude's own aesthetic.

Three design briefs shipped. The question that surfaced in every project, in different words, was the same one: how do you make this look like it wasn't made by AI?

## Naučimo Hrvatski, scene by scene.

Marin generated three directions: Memory Press, Tender Thread, Adriatic Dusk. He picked the third. Two rounds of mockups later, a type system locked, and the first real scene screen built: Scena 3 · Sarma kod bake. Full-bleed warm amber background. Playfair Display italic at display scale. A frosted glass narration card. The line on the card: *Bakina kuhinja miriše na nedjelju, na dim i na sarmu.* That sentence is the project working as intended. One specific kitchen, one specific memory.

The friction through the session was the typeface. Claude kept defaulting away from DM Sans for the UI layer, even after the brief defined it explicitly. It took a direct correction and a round of uploaded references to hold the line. By the end, the direction was settled and Marin was the most satisfied person in the room.

## Wattlog, rewritten from the brief up.

Ivo arrived with a thorough brief already written. It produced a dashboard he didn't like. The brief described the desired look in detail: palette, type scale, component specs. The output followed those specs and still felt generic. Warm color, clean type, nice hierarchy, completely forgettable.

The rewrite changed the approach. Instead of describing what the dashboard should look like, the new brief defined what it couldn't be. Specific type pairing instead of a single safe typeface. Color restricted to a strict signal role: orange, twice, for one reason each. Layout as a ruled ledger, not cards. Charts drawn directly in canvas, not from a library. Each of those instructions blocks a default the model would otherwise reach for. The result looked different not because the palette changed, but because the structural logic changed.

## ClassArcade, three sessions and a rewrite.

Paula's session produced three HTML prototype iterations. The first version: dark background, pixel font, scanline overlay. Rejected immediately. The second and third moved toward warm cream ground, Nunito, vibrant pink and blue and orange for the ranking colors. A 13-item banned moves list written into the brief. Paula committed to scope: a student list and a top-3 leaderboard.

Then the session continued past the brief.

Three more build attempts kept producing the same dashboard. Rounded Nunito everything. Saturated cards. Emoji avatars with thick rings. The brief was being followed. The output was still generic. Adding more bans made it worse. Listing what to avoid confirmed those patterns were in scope.

Two things changed in the session that broke it: the reference images and the brief. Two children's-app screenshots from early on had set the visual register for everything that followed. Swapping them for editorial references (a kids site with Fraunces italic headlines, white ground, type-led hierarchy) produced a different output on the first attempt. The new brief changed what it was allowed to produce.

The other technique that worked: side-by-side comparison. Telling the model "this is too generic" got polite agreement and another generic result. Pasting both outputs and asking it to name the specific differences produced an analysis specific enough to act on. Four words: *"Compare the differences and write down the findings."*

## After the session, what worked and why.

The improved designs weren't better because the briefs were more detailed. They were more restrictive in the right places.

A prompt that behaves like a mood board gives the model room to fill the gaps with whatever is statistically common. A prompt that behaves like a design system closes those gaps before the model reaches for defaults.

What that looks like in practice: start with the product job, not the aesthetic. Define a governing idea specific enough that it can't be averaged: "Braun hardware logic," not "clean and playful." Then set hard constraints on typeface, color roles, layout patterns, and component structure. Then name the AI defaults you're blocking, and give a replacement for each one. A bare ban describes a problem. A replacement gives the model something to do instead. A checklist at the end makes regression visible: is Fraunces italic loaded? Are XP numbers in the right weight? Does the headline mix both fonts?

That structure produced a different class of output. Product job, governing idea, hard constraints, blacklist with replacements, checklist.

## The session, looking back.

All three projects closed with a brief. Marin's was the one he wanted. Ivo's and Paula's led somewhere less expected. The question turned into more work, and the work held up.

Marin locked his direction in 45 minutes. He was the only one who didn't spend the session fighting the aesthetic. His read: if the product works, the look is secondary. "I'm happy as long as the app works and performs well."
