Claude went down ten minutes into the session and kept dropping in and out for the rest of the afternoon. When the API was up we worked the brief. When it was down we talked through the projects and googled references the way we used to. Nobody panicked. The afternoon ended on time.

By the end of it, every project had a written brief and three of the four had moved in some material way.

## Wattlog, on paper.

Ivo's project picked up its name in session 02. The brief came in three pieces: a competitive matrix across eleven training tools, a sixteen-feature priority list ranked P0 through P3, and a not-building list that opens with "Strava exists" before ruling out coaching software, video workouts, route planners, and four other things Wattlog could be mistaken for. The brief didn't change Ivo's direction. It got the direction onto paper, where it can be argued with, which is the only thing a brief is really for.

The shape of the project is settled. The visual side isn't. That's session 03's problem.

## The Mockup Generator, in three drafts.

The brief went through three drafts inside the two hours of session 02. Ivan kept asking Claude to argue with what it had just produced. Twice it did, persuasively. Tap-to-refine got cut from v1 and replaced with a lock-and-shuffle pattern. The input hierarchy was restructured so two dimensions are primary and four collapse by default. Background removal moved from a post-generation tool into one of the choices inside Environment. GPT Image 2 was confirmed as the generation API after Ivan asked Claude to check the current state of the field in real time.

The product Ivan is building is a wrapper that turns six visual choices into a prompt the user never has to write. The quote at the top of this entry is the line he picked to describe his own design rule.

He also said, near the end of the session, that he's scared of the API connection. He isn't the only one in that boat. The Mockup Generator runs on GPT Image 2. Marin's app already calls Claude Sonnet 4. Paula's tracker will want an AI summary layer eventually. The people building these aren't engineers. The work of making API plumbing feel ordinary is going to be its own small thread across the next few sessions.

## Naučimo Hrvatski, reframed.

Marin came into session 02 with the FaceTime-style conversation app he'd built in session 01. He left with a completely different project.

The shift came from a research check. Ten conversation-based language apps surveyed, and none of them are built around a couple's actual relationship. Once that gap was visible, the FaceTime app stopped being the right shape.

The new project is *Naučimo Hrvatski* as a memory book. Six chapters mapped to real trips Marin and Kenzie have taken, December 2024 through her next visit in September 2026. Each chapter opens at the airport. Each scene inside is a day or a moment from the trip: Marin's grandma's house, the walk by the Drava, New Year's in Rome. To turn a page, Kenzie has to do something in Croatian. Describe a photo. Fill in a sentence she would have said at the time. Retell the day. The chapter closes with her retelling the whole trip in Croatian, the AI gently correcting, and the retelling saved into the book as her own version of the story.

The decision Marin still has to make is how many chapters to ship by week 12. Claude's recommendation is one chapter fully built, handed over as a working gift, then more chapters drop in over time. The alternative is six chapters half-built by the end of the program, which is a deck. He hasn't picked yet.

## The Classroom Behavior Tracker, narrowed.

Paula spent the first half of the session moving between project ideas. She spent the second half committing. The Classroom Behavior Tracker is now a teacher-only tool, built in Claude Code, for one specific teacher Paula knows.

The two scope cuts she made in the same hour will shape everything downstream. Students never interact with the app. They only see a projected scoreboard. Negative consequences happen offline, not on the screen. The screen is the reward layer. Punishment lives in real life.

The aesthetic is pixel-art and gamified. Habitica is the starting reference, with Duolingo's tiered leagues borrowed for the leaderboard pattern. Before any visible ranking gets designed, Paula wants to read the PBIS literature on public point displays. The psychology side of "who can see whose score" is going to shape what the student view actually becomes, and that's worth thinking through before the pixels start moving.

## The session, looking back.

Three of the four projects moved materially. Ivan's brief through three drafts. Marin's whole concept reframed by one research check. Paula's two scope cuts. Ivo's project picked up a name and eight items on the not-building list. Nobody walked out with the same project they walked in with, except possibly Ivo, whose project was already converged before the day started.

The outage was the day's quiet character. It came back enough times to be a presence, and not enough times to be a disaster. We worked around it. That's probably the most useful thing we practiced today.

## Between sessions, the plan got rewritten.

After two sessions we had enough real participant work to see what the program actually needs versus what we'd assumed at the planning stage. Between session 02 and session 03, the facilitator did a full revision pass on the workshop plan.

Some sessions changed. Some topics shifted. Parts that had felt necessary on paper but weren't earning their place in the room came out. The remaining sessions are tighter, with more room for participants to plan ahead, test, and review work properly.

The prompts got rewritten. Each session now has its own set that match what the session is actually asking participants to do, rather than the generic versions the plan started with. A new pre-prompt challenge called *Think Before You Prompt* sits at the top of every session. It frames how to talk to Claude before opening the chat.

Each session now also names the Claude model and mode it expects. We're running every session in its own chat, so picking the right Claude for the work matters.

The journal got a redesign. A contents side panel makes the entries easier to navigate as more land. The voice the AI uses to draft entries got tightened too, so the posts capture what the session was actually like instead of reading as polished but empty.

The facilitator also moved his own work into Claude Cowork to see how the desktop-agent version handles more complex agentic tasks. The personal-Jarvis test, with the workshop as the workload.
