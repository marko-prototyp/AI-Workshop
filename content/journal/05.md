The prototypes were ugly, and that was the plan. Plain boxes, almost no color, default fonts. For the first time, though, you could click them.

After a week off, Paula, Ivo and Marin each turned their wireframes into a single clickable HTML file, just to find out whether the thing holds up once you move through it. Fable 5 had launched days earlier, so this was its first run here too. First drafts came out buggy, and most people ran three versions before the flows settled.

The move that made the session was the same for all three: build it rough, then walk through it as the person who would really use it. Ivo walked his as himself on a Tuesday night, Marin as Kenzie, his beginner, Paula as the teacher mid-class. A wireframe tells you the screens exist. Walking a working version tells you whether the idea behind them holds, and every decision that mattered came out of that gap.

## Wattlog, the loop that never closes.

Ivo built all nine screens into one file you can click end to end, with fake numbers that hold together: an FTP of 284 gives a threshold prescription of 270 to 298 watts, so nothing on screen contradicts anything else.

<artifact slug="week-05/ivo-wattlog"></artifact>

Then he ran the prompt of the session: *"Walk through my artifact as if you were me. Don’t critique the design. Critique the experience."* Claude played it back as a real Tuesday-evening upload and found the one thing that matters. You upload a ride and your prescription doesn’t change. The loop never closes.

That sounds minor. It’s the whole project. Wattlog exists for its prescriptive layer, the part that reads your training and tells you what to do next. If an upload changes nothing on screen, it’s a logbook with a nicer skin, or in Ivo’s words "a static poster of the concept." So the next move is clear: before any styling, the build has to make the prescription respond to at least one upload. Ivo is leaving the styling for later, which is the right order.

One smaller moment: before building, Claude caught that the wireframes and the brief disagreed about the Today screen and asked which to follow instead of guessing. Cheaper to fix then than later. Ivo’s own worst moment was blunter: "Bad styling :D".

## Naučimo Hrvatski, walked through as Kenzie.

Marin’s flow works end to end, from the recap to the finish screen. Two corrections reshaped it, and both protect the one thing that makes this not a generic language app: it’s built around his and Kenzie’s real relationship, a memory book of their trips.

<artifact slug="week-05/naucimo-hrvatski-smoketest"></artifact>

The first was the cast. Claude kept inventing characters who don’t exist, so Marin locked it to two people and cut the rest, repeating "No Marko, just Marin here." It sounds like a nitpick, but the moment a stranger walks into the story, the app stops being theirs.

The second was the interaction. Every screen moved from typing to dragging a word into a blank. Typing Croatian, diacritics and all, is too hard for a beginner, and the project has a rule against making Kenzie feel bad. Dragging keeps the learning and drops the wall. It also removed a planning headache: with no free typing, there’s no Croatian to validate at runtime.

Walking it as Kenzie exposed the gaps. A wrong answer stops her but never teaches her. She can’t flip back to an earlier photo. The vocabulary list unlocks only after she’s past needing it. Nothing tells her the app saved her place. And the chapter just ends. That list is the spec for the next version, and Marin has already picked the 11 photos for Chapter 1 and written a line for each.

## ClassArcade, three fixes and an honest brief.

Paula built the whole app into one clickable file on the real rosters, and ran it three times. Then she walked it as the teacher mid-class and found three problems, all about trust rather than looks.

<artifact slug="week-05/classarcade-smoke-test"></artifact>

Giving one point to one student was the slowest thing in the app, which is backwards, since it’s what the teacher does all day. So every row got plus and minus buttons. A point you tapped couldn’t be undone, and the board is projected to the class, so a wrong tap is a public mistake. So she added a one-level undo. And the confirmation flash got lost when the list re-sorted before you saw it, so it now plays before the board rearranges.

She also dropped the 30-second delete timer for one rule: nothing is real until you Save. A countdown just makes you anxious. The save model lets you experiment freely, and nothing commits until you decide.

The harder problem was scope. Claude had quietly added some things nobody asked for. That was Paula’s worst moment, "Claude adding some things that weren’t specified anywhere." Instead of keeping them by default, she wrote a short reconciliation brief that lines up the plan against the build, decides what stays, and re-locks scope. It’s honest about what got worse, too: the podium ended up stacked like a shorter copy of the student list, so the leaderboard lost its one moment. Recovering that is the visual work she’s heading into next.

The build also left one question open. ClassArcade now has a minus button and per-category tallies, and the project runs on a "calm, not surveillance" idea. Whether either belongs on the screen the whole class sees is the first thing for next time. As Paula put it, "I want to give the project more time to make it really good."

## What the session was really about.

Three rough prototypes, and every one held up enough to prove the brief behind it is a real product. Where they fell short, walking them as the user showed exactly how. That’s the shift this week: the brief stopped being something you argue about and became something you can test.

It changed how people feel about the work, too. A few want to revise their briefs after seeing them move, and for the first time almost everyone said they’d keep building at home, outside the hour.

This was also the first session where the group reviewed each other’s work, clicking through prototypes that weren’t theirs and agreeing on a few shared UX rules along the way. It needed more steering than usual, but the back-and-forth was the best part of the day. Ivan wasn’t here again.
